<h2 id="mp-tfa-h2" class="mp-h2"><span id="From_today.27s_featured_article"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today's_featured_article">From today's featured article</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfa"><div id="mp-tfa-img" style="float: left; margin: 0.5em 0.9em 0.4em 0em;">
<div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 162px;">
<a href="/wiki/File:Vicki_Cole.jpg" class="image" title="Cole with a recreation of her sign at the 1969 inaugural parade"><img alt="Cole with a recreation of her sign at the 1969 inaugural parade" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Vicki_Cole.jpg/162px-Vicki_Cole.jpg" decoding="async" width="162" height="122" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Vicki_Cole.jpg/243px-Vicki_Cole.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Vicki_Cole.jpg/324px-Vicki_Cole.jpg 2x" data-file-width="720" data-file-height="540" /></a><div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Cole with a recreation of her sign at the 1969 inaugural parade</div></div>
</div>
<p>"<b><a href="/wiki/Bring_Us_Together" title="Bring Us Together">Bring Us Together</a></b>" was a <a href="/wiki/List_of_political_slogans" title="List of political slogans">political slogan</a> popularized after the election of <a href="/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States)" title="Republican Party (United States)">Republican</a> candidate <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a> as <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">President of the United States</a> in <a href="/wiki/1968_United_States_presidential_election" title="1968 United States presidential election">the 1968 election</a>. The text was derived from a sign that 13-year-old Vicki Lynne Cole stated that she had carried at Nixon's rally in her home town of <a href="/wiki/Deshler,_Ohio" title="Deshler, Ohio">Deshler, Ohio</a>, during the campaign. After being told of the sign, Nixon's speechwriters, including <a href="/wiki/William_Safire" title="William Safire">William Safire</a>, began inserting the phrase into his speeches. Nixon mentioned the rally sign in his victory speech, adopting the phrase as representing his administration's initial goal—to reunify the bitterly divided country. Nixon invited Cole and her family to <a href="/wiki/First_inauguration_of_Richard_Nixon" title="First inauguration of Richard Nixon">the presidential inauguration</a>, and she appeared on a <a href="/wiki/Float_(parade)" title="Float (parade)">float</a> in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_presidential_inauguration#Inaugural_parade" title="United States presidential inauguration">inaugural parade</a> <i>(pictured)</i>. The phrase "Bring Us Together" was used ironically by <a href="/wiki/Democratic_Party_(United_States)" title="Democratic Party (United States)">Democrats</a> when Nixon proposed policies with which they disagreed. In newspaper columns written in the final years before his 2009 death, Safire expressed doubt that Cole's sign had ever existed. <h2 id="mp-tfl-h2" class="mp-h2"><span id="From_today.27s_featured_list"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="From_today's_featured_list">From today's featured list</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfl"><div id="mp-tfl-img" style="float:right;margin:0.5em 0 0.4em 0.9em;"><div class="thumbinner mp-thumb" style="background: transparent; border: none; padding: 0; max-width: 135px;">
<a href="/wiki/File:Raymond_Chandler_(Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait,_1943).jpg" class="image" title="Raymond Chandler"><img alt="Raymond Chandler" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Raymond_Chandler_%28Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait%2C_1943%29.jpg/135px-Raymond_Chandler_%28Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait%2C_1943%29.jpg" decoding="async" width="135" height="146" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Raymond_Chandler_%28Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait%2C_1943%29.jpg/203px-Raymond_Chandler_%28Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait%2C_1943%29.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Raymond_Chandler_%28Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait%2C_1943%29.jpg/270px-Raymond_Chandler_%28Lady_in_the_Lake_portrait%2C_1943%29.jpg 2x" data-file-width="361" data-file-height="391" /></a><div class="thumbcaption" style="padding: 0.25em 0; word-wrap: break-word;">Raymond Chandler</div></div>
</div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler" title="Raymond Chandler">Raymond Chandler</a>, an American-British novelist and screenwriter, <b><a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler_bibliography" title="Raymond Chandler bibliography">published 25 short stories, five novels, and a number of poems and scripts</a></b>.  These include the 22 short stories he wrote between 1933 and 1941. In the mid-1940s, some of the first 22 began appearing in inexpensive paperback and hardcover collections published by <a href="/wiki/Avon_Books" class="mw-redirect" title="Avon Books">Avon Books</a> and <a href="/wiki/World_Publishing_Company" title="World Publishing Company">World Publishing Company</a>. Previously he had worked as a journalist and writer—mostly for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Westminster_Gazette" title="The Westminster Gazette">The Westminster Gazette</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Academy_(periodical)" title="The Academy (periodical)">The Academy</a></i>. In 1950, <a href="/wiki/Houghton_Mifflin_Harcourt" title="Houghton Mifflin Harcourt">Houghton Mifflin</a> published the hardcover collection <i><a href="/wiki/The_Simple_Art_of_Murder" title="The Simple Art of Murder">The Simple Art of Murder</a></i>, containing a dozen stories selected by Chandler and an essay on mystery stories. Eight stories that he had "cannibalized" (his term) while writing his novels were omitted at his request. After Chandler's death, these eight were published in <i><a href="/wiki/Killer_in_the_Rain" title="Killer in the Rain">Killer in the Rain</a></i> (1964). In 1939, at the behest of the publisher <a href="/wiki/Alfred_A._Knopf_Sr." title="Alfred A. Knopf Sr.">Alfred A. Knopf&#160;Sr.</a>, Chandler wrote his first novel, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Big_Sleep" title="The Big Sleep">The Big Sleep</a></i>, for which he used parts of his short stories "Killer in the Rain" (1935) and "The Curtain" (1936). In 1944 Chandler was asked by <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount Pictures</a> to write the script for the film <i><a href="/wiki/Double_Indemnity" title="Double Indemnity">Double Indemnity</a></i> with <a href="/wiki/Billy_Wilder" title="Billy Wilder">Billy Wilder</a>; the film was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Best_Adapted_Screenplay" title="Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay">Academy Award for Best Adapted Screenplay</a>. It was the first of seven scripts Chandler wrote, although two of them were unused. (<b><a href="/wiki/Raymond_Chandler_bibliography" title="Raymond Chandler bibliography">Full&#160;list...</a></b>)
</p>

<h2 id="mp-tfp-h2" class="mp-h2"><span id="Today.27s_featured_picture"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Today's_featured_picture">Today's featured picture</span></h2>
<div id="mp-tfp">
<table role="presentation" style="margin:0 3px 3px; width:100%; box-sizing:border-box; text-align:left; background-color:transparent; border-collapse:collapse;">
<tbody><tr>
<td style="padding:0 0.9em 0 0; width:300px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg" class="image" title="Book of Tobit"><img alt="Book of Tobit" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg/300px-Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg" decoding="async" width="300" height="422" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg/450px-Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/30/Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg/600px-Tobias_and_the_Angel_-_Filippino_Lippi.jpg 2x" data-file-width="5529" data-file-height="7769" /></a>

<td style="padding:0 6px 0 0">
<p>The <b><a href="/wiki/Book_of_Tobit" title="Book of Tobit">Book of Tobit</a></b> is a Jewish work from the 3rd or early 2nd century BCE describing how God tests the faithful, responds to prayers, and protects the covenant community (the Israelites). It is regarded as part of the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_canon" title="Biblical canon">biblical canon</a> of the Catholic and Orthodox churches as a <a href="/wiki/Deuterocanonical_books" title="Deuterocanonical books">deuterocanonical book</a>, but as part of the <a href="/wiki/Biblical_apocrypha" title="Biblical apocrypha">biblical apocrypha</a> in some Protestant churches. This 15th-century oil-on-panel painting by <a href="/wiki/Filippino_Lippi" title="Filippino Lippi">Filippino Lippi</a>, entitled <i>Tobias and the Angel</i>, depicts a scene in which Tobias, Tobit's son, goes on a journey accompanied by an angel, without realising that he is an angel, and is instructed what to do with the giant fish that he catches.
</p>
