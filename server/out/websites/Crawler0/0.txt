<p>The <b>National Firearms Act</b> (<b>NFA</b>), <a href="/wiki/73rd_United_States_Congress" title="73rd United States Congress">73rd Congress</a>, Sess. 2, ch. 757, 48&#160;<a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">Stat.</a>&#160;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://legislink.org/us/stat-48-1236">1236</a> was enacted on June 26, 1934, and currently codified and amended as <a href="/wiki/Internal_Revenue_Code" title="Internal Revenue Code">I.R.C.</a>&#32;<a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="https://www.law.cornell.edu/uscode/text/26/subtitle-E/chapter-53">ch. 53</a>. The law is an <a href="/wiki/Act_of_Congress" title="Act of Congress">Act of Congress</a> in the United States that, in general, imposes an <a href="/wiki/Excise" title="Excise">excise</a> tax on the manufacture and transfer of <a href="/wiki/Title_II_weapons" title="Title II weapons">certain firearms</a> and mandates the registration of those firearms. The NFA is also referred to as <a href="/wiki/Title_II_weapons" title="Title II weapons">Title II</a> of the federal firearms laws, with the <a href="/wiki/Gun_Control_Act_of_1968" title="Gun Control Act of 1968">Gun Control Act of 1968</a> ("GCA") is Title I.
</p><p>All transfers of ownership of registered NFA firearms must be done through the National Firearms Registration and Transfer Record (the "NFA registry").<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup> The NFA also requires that the permanent transport of NFA firearms across state lines by the owner must be reported to the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives</a> (ATF). Temporary transports of some items, most notably suppressors, do not need to be reported.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The ostensible impetus for the National Firearms Act of 1934 was the <a href="/wiki/Organized_crime" title="Organized crime">gangland crime</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Prohibition_era" class="mw-redirect" title="Prohibition era">Prohibition era</a>, such as the <a href="/wiki/Saint_Valentine%27s_Day_Massacre" title="Saint Valentine&#39;s Day Massacre">St. Valentine's Day Massacre</a> of 1929, and the <a href="/wiki/Giuseppe_Zangara#Assassination_attempt" title="Giuseppe Zangara">attempted assassination</a> of President-elect <a href="/wiki/Franklin_D._Roosevelt" title="Franklin D. Roosevelt">Franklin D. Roosevelt</a> in 1933.<sup id="cite_ref-MoralControversiesp175_3-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-MoralControversiesp175-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:<span>824</span></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ATFHistory_5-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATFHistory-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ATFHist-Oxford_6-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATFHist-Oxford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup> Like the current National Firearms Act (NFA), the 1934 Act required NFA firearms to be registered and taxed. The $200 tax was quite prohibitive at the time (equivalent to $3,869&#32;in 2020). With a few exceptions, the tax amount is unchanged.<sup id="cite_ref-ATFHistory_5-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATFHistory-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-ATFHist-Oxford_6-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-ATFHist-Oxford-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Originally, pistols and revolvers were to be regulated as strictly as machine guns; towards that end, cutting down a rifle or shotgun to circumvent the handgun restrictions by making a concealable weapon was taxed as strictly as a machine gun.<sup id="cite_ref-Carter2002_7-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carter2002-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Conventional pistols and revolvers were ultimately excluded from the Act before passage, but other concealable weapons were not.<sup id="cite_ref-Carter2002_7-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Carter2002-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup> Regarding the definition of "firearm," the language of the statute as originally enacted was as follows:
</p>
<dl><dd><dl><dd>The term "firearm" means a shotgun or rifle having a barrel of less than eighteen inches in length, or any other weapon, except a pistol or revolver, from which a shot is discharged by an explosive if such weapon is capable of being concealed on the person, or a machine gun, and includes a muffler or silencer for any firearm whether or not such firearm is included within the foregoing definition.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup></dd></dl></dd></dl>
<p>Under the original Act, NFA weapons were machine guns, short-barreled rifles (SBR), short-barreled shotguns (SBS), any other weapons (AOW, i.e., concealable weapons other than pistols or revolvers), and silencers for any type of NFA or non-NFA weapon. Minimum barrel length was soon amended to 16&#160;inches for rimfire rifles and by 1960 had been amended to 16&#160;inches for centerfire rifles as well.<sup id="cite_ref-Appropriations1998_9-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Appropriations1998-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>NFA categories have been modified by laws passed by Congress, rulings by the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Treasury" title="United States Department of the Treasury">Department of the Treasury</a>, and regulations promulgated by the enforcement agency assigned, known as the Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives or ATF.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Categories_of_regulated_firearms">Categories of regulated firearms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Categories of regulated firearms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Title_II_weapons" title="Title II weapons">Title II weapons</a></div>
<p>The National Firearms Act of 1934 (NFA) defines a number of categories of regulated firearms. These weapons are collectively known as NFA firearms and include the following:
</p>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">Machine guns</a></dt>
<dd>Includes any firearm which can fire repeatedly, without manual reloading, "by a single function of the trigger",<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup> Both continuous fully automatic fire and "<a href="/wiki/Burst_fire" class="mw-redirect" title="Burst fire">burst fire</a>" (e.g., firearms with a 3-round burst feature) are considered machine gun features. The weapon's receiver is by itself considered to be a regulated firearm. A non-machine gun that may be converted to fire more than one shot per trigger pull by ordinary mechanical skills is determined to be "readily convertible", and classed as a machine gun, such as a <a href="/wiki/KG-9" class="mw-redirect" title="KG-9">KG-9</a> pistol (pre-ban ones are "grandfathered").<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup></dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Short-barreled_rifle" title="Short-barreled rifle">Short-barreled rifles</a> (SBRs)</dt>
<dd>Includes any firearm with a buttstock and either a rifled barrel less than 16" long or an overall length under 26". The overall length is measured with any folding or collapsing stocks in the extended position. The category also includes firearms which came from the factory with a buttstock that was later removed by a third party.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Sawed-off_shotgun" title="Sawed-off shotgun">Short barreled shotguns</a> (SBSs)</dt>
<dd>Similarly to SBRs, but with either a <a href="/wiki/Smoothbore" title="Smoothbore">smoothbore</a> barrel less than 18" long or a minimum overall length under 26".</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Suppressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Suppressor">Suppressors</a></dt>
<dd>The legal term for a suppressor is <i>silencer</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> and includes any portable device designed to muffle or disguise the report of a portable firearm, but does not include non-portable devices, such as sound traps used by <a href="/wiki/Gunsmiths" class="mw-redirect" title="Gunsmiths">gunsmiths</a> in their shops which are large and usually bolted to the floor.</dd></dl>
<dl><dt><a href="/wiki/Destructive_device" title="Destructive device">Destructive devices</a> (DDs) - (added to the NFA of 1934 by the <a href="/wiki/Omnibus_Crime_Control_and_Safe_Streets_Act_of_1968" title="Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968">Omnibus Crime Control and Safe Streets Act of 1968</a>)</dt>
<dd>There are two broad classes of destructive devices<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>: <div><ul><li>Devices such as <a href="/wiki/Grenade" title="Grenade">grenades</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bomb" title="Bomb">bombs</a>, explosive <a href="/wiki/Missile" title="Missile">missiles</a>, <a href="/wiki/Poison_gas" class="mw-redirect" title="Poison gas">poison gas</a> weapons, etc.</li><li>Any firearm with a bore over 0.50 inch except for shotguns or shotgun shells which have been found to be generally recognized as particularly suitable for sporting purposes. (Many firearms with bores over 0.50 inch, such as 10-gauge or 12-gauge shotguns, are exempted from the law because they have been determined to have a "legitimate sporting use".)</li></ul></div></dd></dl>
<h3><span id="Any_other_weapon_.28AOW.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Any_other_weapon_(AOW)">Any other weapon (AOW)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Any other weapon (AOW)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Firearms meeting the definition of "any other weapon", or AOW, are weapons or devices that can be concealed on the person and from which a shot can be discharged by the energy of an explosive. Many AOWs are disguised devices such as <a href="/wiki/Pen_gun" title="Pen gun">pens</a>, cigarette lighters, knives, cane guns, and umbrella guns. AOWs can be pistols and revolvers with smooth bore barrels (e.g., <a href="/wiki/H%26R_Handy-Gun" title="H&amp;R Handy-Gun">H&amp;R Handy-Gun</a>, <a href="/wiki/Serbu_Super-Shorty" title="Serbu Super-Shorty">Serbu Super-Shorty</a>) designed or redesigned to fire a fixed shotgun shell. While the above weapons are similar in appearance to weapons made from shotguns, they were originally manufactured in the described configuration rather than modified from existing shotguns. As a result, such weapons do not fit within the definition of shotgun or weapons made from a shotgun.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2017)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>The AOW definition includes specifically described weapons with combination shotgun and rifle barrels 12&#160;inches or more but less than 18&#160;inches in length from which only a single discharge can be made from either barrel without manual reloading.
</p><p>The ATF Firearms Technology Branch has issued opinions that when a pistol (such as an AR-type pistol) under 26" in overall length is fitted with a vertical fore-grip, it is no longer "designed, made and intended to fire ... when held in one hand," and therefore no longer meets the definition of a pistol.  Such a firearm then falls only within the definition of "any other weapon" under the NFA.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In 1938 Congress recognized that the <a href="/wiki/Marble_Game_Getter" title="Marble Game Getter">Marble Game Getter</a>, a short <a href="/wiki/.22LR" class="mw-redirect" title=".22LR">.22</a>/<a href="/wiki/.410" class="mw-redirect" title=".410">.410</a> sporting firearm, had "legitimate use" and did not deserve the stigma of a "gangster weapon" and reduced the $200 tax to one dollar for the Game Getter. In 1960 Congress changed the transfer tax for all AOW category firearms to $5. The transfer tax for machine guns, silencers, SBR and SBS remained at $200.<sup id="cite_ref-Wilson1997_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Wilson1997-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Parts_associated_with_NFA_items">Parts associated with NFA items</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Parts associated with NFA items">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In general, certain components that make up an NFA item are considered regulated. For example, the components of a silencer are considered as "silencers" by themselves and the replacement parts are regulated. However, the repair of original parts without replacement can be done by the original manufacturer, FFL gunsmith, or by registered owner without being subjected to new registration as long as the serial number and the dimension (caliber) are maintained.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14">&#91;14&#93;</a></sup> The length may be reduced in repair, but cannot be increased. Increasing the length is considered as making a new silencer. "Suppressor" is the term used within the trade/industry literature while the term "silencer" is the commonly used term that appears in the actual wording of the NFA. The terms are often used interchangeably depending on the source quoted.
</p><p>Suppressors and machine guns are the most heavily regulated. For example, in Ruling 81-4, ATF declared that any AR-15 Drop-in Auto-Sear (DIAS) made after November 1, 1981 is itself a machine gun, and is therefore subject to regulation.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15">&#91;15&#93;</a></sup> While this might seem to mean that pre-1981 sears are legal to possess without registration, ATF closes this loophole in other publications, stating, "<i>Regardless of the date of manufacture of a drop in auto sear, possession of such a sear and certain M-16 fire control parts is possession of a machine gun as defined by the NFA. Specifically, these parts are listed as "(a) combination(s) of parts" designed "Solely and exclusively" for use in converting a weapon into a machine gun and are a machine gun as defined in the NFA.</i>" ATF machine gun technology letters written between 1980 and 1996 by Edward M. Owen – the then-chief of the ATF technology division defined "solely and exclusively" in all of his published and unpublished machine gun rulings with specific non-ambiguous language.<sup id="cite_ref-Sweeney20132_16-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Sweeney20132-16">&#91;16&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Owning the parts needed to assemble other NFA firearms is generally restricted. One individual cannot own or manufacture certain machine gun sear (fire-control) components unless he owns a registered machine gun. The <a href="/wiki/M2_carbine" class="mw-redirect" title="M2 carbine">M2 carbine</a> trigger pack is such an example of a "combination of parts" that is a machine gun in and of itself. Most of these have been registered as they were pulled from stores of surplus rifles in the early 1960s. In some special cases, exceptions have been determined to these rules by ATF. A semiautomatic firearm which could have a string or shoelace looped around the cocking handle of and then behind and in front of the trigger in such a way as to allow the firearm to be fired automatically is no longer considered a machine gun unless the string is attached in this manner.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17">&#91;17&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Most current fully automatic trigger groups will not fit their <a href="/wiki/Semi-automatic_firearm" title="Semi-automatic firearm">semi-automatic firearm</a> look-alike counterparts – the semi-automatic version is specifically constructed to reject the fully automatic trigger group by adding metal in critical places. This addition is required by ATF to prevent easy conversion of Title I firearms into machine guns.
</p><p>For civilian possession, all machine guns must have been manufactured and registered with ATF prior to May 19, 1986 to be transferable between citizens.<sup id="cite_ref-Kleck_18-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Kleck-18">&#91;18&#93;</a></sup> These machine gun prices have drastically escalated in value, especially items like registered sears and conversion-kits. Only a Class-II manufacturer (a FFL holder licensed to manufacture firearms or Type-07 license that has paid a Special Occupational Tax Stamp or SOT) could manufacture machine guns after that date, and they can only be sold to government, law-enforcement, and military entities. Transfer can only be done to other SOT FFL-holders, and such FFL-holders must have a "demonstration letter" from a respective government agency to receive such machine guns.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19">&#91;19&#93;</a></sup> Falsification and/or misuse of the "demo-letter" process can and has resulted in long jail sentences and felony convictions for violators.
</p><p>Owning both a short barrel and a legal-length rifle could be construed as intent to build an illegal, unregistered SBR. This possibility was contested and won in the U.S. Supreme Court case of <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Thompson-Center_Arms_Company" class="mw-redirect" title="United States v. Thompson-Center Arms Company">United States v. Thompson-Center Arms Company</a></i>. ATF lost the case, and was unable to prove that possession of a short barrel for the specific pistol configuration of a Thompson Contender is illegal. ATF later released ruling 2011-4<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20">&#91;20&#93;</a></sup> to clarify the legal status of owning such conversion kits.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21">&#91;21&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Removal of a weapon from classification as an NFA firearm, such as the reclassification of the original <a href="/wiki/Broomhandle_Mauser" class="mw-redirect" title="Broomhandle Mauser">Broomhandle Mauser</a> with shoulder stock from "short barrel rifle" (SBR) to a <a href="/wiki/Collectors_of_Curio_and_Relic_Firearms" class="mw-redirect" title="Collectors of Curio and Relic Firearms">curio or relic</a> handgun, changed its status as a Title II NFA firearm but did not change its status as a Title I Gun Control Act firearm.<sup id="cite_ref-Magaw1996_22-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Magaw1996-22">&#91;22&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Muzzle-loading firearms are exempt from the Act (as they are defined as "antique firearms" and are not considered "firearms" under either the GCA or the NFA). Thus, though common muzzle-loading hunting rifles are available in calibers over 0.50 inch, they are not regulated as destructive devices. Muzzle-loading cannon are similarly exempt since the law makes no distinction about the size of muzzle-loading weapons. Thus it is legal for a civilian to build muzzle-loading rifles, pistols, cannon, and mortars with no paperwork. However, ammunition for these weapons can still be classified as destructive devices themselves, such as explosive shells. While an 'antique firearm' is not considered a 'firearm' under the NFA, some states (such as Oregon) have laws that specifically prohibit anyone that could not otherwise own/obtain an GCA or NFA defined 'firearm' (i.e., felons, recipients of dishonorable discharge from military service, the mentally adjudicated, etc.) from owning/obtaining an 'antique firearm'.<sup id="cite_ref-Flayderman2007_23-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Flayderman2007-23">&#91;23&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Individuals or companies seeking to market large-bore firearms may apply to ATF for a "sporting clause exception". If granted, ATF acknowledges that the firearm has a legitimate sporting use and is therefore not a destructive device. Certain large <a href="/wiki/Big_Five_game" class="mw-redirect" title="Big Five game">safari rifle</a> calibers, such as <a href="/wiki/.585_Nyati" title=".585 Nyati">.585 Nyati</a> and <a href="/wiki/.577_Tyrannosaur" title=".577 Tyrannosaur">.577 Tyrannosaur</a>, have such exceptions.
</p><p>The phrase "all NFA rules apply" is commonplace. This disclaimer is usually posted in bold print from firearm dealers holding an FFL license.
</p>
<h2><span id="Registration.2C_purchases.2C_taxes_and_transfers"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Registration,_purchases,_taxes_and_transfers">Registration, purchases, taxes and transfers</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Registration, purchases, taxes and transfers">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It is a common misconception<sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24">&#91;24&#93;</a></sup> that an individual must have a "Class 3" license in order to own an NFA weapon. A <a href="/wiki/Federal_Firearms_License" title="Federal Firearms License">Federal Firearms License</a> (FFL) is not required to be an individual owner, although it is required as a prerequisite to become a Special Occupation Taxpayer (SOT, see <a href="/wiki/Special_Occupational_Taxpayers" title="Special Occupational Taxpayers">Special Occupational Taxpayers</a>): Class 1 importer, Class 2 manufacturer-dealer or Class 3 dealer in NFA weapons. There are generally three ways to own a NFA weapon: as an individual, through a <a href="/wiki/Gun_Trust" class="mw-redirect" title="Gun Trust">gun trust</a>, or as a <a href="/wiki/Limited_liability_company" title="Limited liability company">Limited Liability Company (LLC)</a>. Legal possession of an NFA firearm by an individual requires transfer of registration within the NFA registry. An individual owner does not need to be an NFA dealer to buy <a href="/wiki/Title_II_weapons" title="Title II weapons">Title II weapons</a>. The sale and purchase of an NFA weapon is, however, taxed and regulated, as follows:
</p><p>All NFA items must be registered with the <a href="/wiki/Bureau_of_Alcohol,_Tobacco,_Firearms_and_Explosives" title="Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives">Bureau of Alcohol, Tobacco, Firearms and Explosives</a> (ATF). Private owners wishing to purchase an NFA item must obtain approval from the ATF, pass an extensive <a href="/wiki/Background_check" title="Background check">background check</a> to include submitting a photograph and fingerprints, fully register the firearm, receive ATF written permission before moving the firearm across state lines, and pay a tax.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25">&#91;25&#93;</a></sup> The request to transfer ownership of an NFA item is made on an ATF Form 4.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26">&#91;26&#93;</a></sup> There have been several unfavorable lawsuits where plaintiffs have been denied NFA approval for a transfer. These lawsuits include: <i>Lomont v. O'Neill</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-27" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-27">&#91;27&#93;</a></sup> <i>Westfall v. Miller</i>,<sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28">&#91;28&#93;</a></sup> and <i>Steele v. National Branch</i>.<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29">&#91;29&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>NFA items may also be transferred to <a href="/wiki/Corporations" class="mw-redirect" title="Corporations">corporations</a> (or other legal entities such as a trust). When the paperwork to request transfer of an NFA item is initiated by an officer of a corporation, fingerprint cards and photographs of the official need to be submitted with the transfer request.  This method has downsides, since it is the corporation (and not the principal) that owns the firearm. Thus, if the corporation dissolves, it must transfer its NFA weapon to the owners. This event would be considered a new transfer and would be subject to a new transfer tax.<sup id="cite_ref-atf-faq_30-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atf-faq-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:162px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Nfastamp.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Nfastamp.jpg/160px-Nfastamp.jpg" decoding="async" width="160" height="227" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Nfastamp.jpg/240px-Nfastamp.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/f/f2/Nfastamp.jpg/320px-Nfastamp.jpg 2x" data-file-width="360" data-file-height="511" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Nfastamp.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>US National Act Stamp, affixed to transfer forms to indicate tax paid.</div></div></div>
<p>The tax for privately manufacturing any NFA firearm (other than machine guns, which are illegal for individuals to manufacture) is $200. Transferring requires a $200 tax for all NFA weapons except AOWs, for which the transfer tax is $5 (although the manufacturing tax remains $200).<sup id="cite_ref-atf-faq_30-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atf-faq-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>All NFA weapons made by individuals must be legal in the State or municipality where the individual lives. The payment of a $200 "making tax" prior to manufacture of the weapon, although a subsequent transfer of AOWs after they are legally "made" is only $5. Only a Class-II manufacturer (a FFL holder licensed as a "Manufacture of Firearms" or Type-07 license that has paid a Special Occupational Tax Stamp or SOT) can manufacture NFA firearms (other than destructive devices) tax free, but they pay a larger annual tax which ranges from $500 to $1000 to cover manufacturing.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31">&#91;31&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>A Destructive Device manufacturing license (Type-10 FFL) holder can manufacture destructive devices tax-free. However a type-07 license costs $150 for three years –– whereas a Type-10 destructive manufacturing license costs $3000 for three years. Both licenses still require the payment of the $500 (reduced-rate) Special Occupational Tax Stamp or SOT, (or the $1000 full tax) per year to conduct manufacturing of NFA weapons that they are respectively qualified to manufacturer. The SOT "reduced rate" applies to a business whose sales are less than $500,000 per year.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32">&#91;32&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Transferable machine guns made or registered before May 19, 1986 are worth far more than their original, pre-1986 value and items like registered "auto-sears," "lightning-links," trigger-packs, <a href="/wiki/Trunnion" title="Trunnion">trunnions</a>, and other "combination of parts" registered as machine guns before the aforementioned date are often worth nearly as much as a full registered machine gun. For instance, as of September 2008, a transferable <a href="/wiki/M16_rifle" title="M16 rifle">M16 rifle</a> costs approximately $11,000 to $18,000, while a transferable "lightning-link" for the <a href="/wiki/AR-15" class="mw-redirect" title="AR-15">AR-15</a> can sell for $8,000 to $10,000. New manufacture M-16s sell to law enforcement and the military for around $600 to $1000.<sup id="cite_ref-33" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-33">&#91;33&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>Upon the request of any ATF agent or investigator, or the Attorney General, the registered owner must provide proof of registration of the firearm.<sup id="cite_ref-34" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-34">&#91;34&#93;</a></sup>
</p><p>In a number of situations, an NFA item may be transferred without a transfer tax. These include sales to government agencies, temporary transfers of an NFA firearm to a gunsmith for repairs, and transfer of an NFA firearm to a lawful heir after the death of its owner. A permanent transfer, even if tax-free, must be approved by ATF. The proper form should be submitted to ATF before the transfer occurs. For example, lawful heirs must submit a Form 5 and wait for approval before taking possession of any NFA item willed to them. Temporary transfers, such as those to a gunsmith or to the original manufacturer for repair, are not subject to ATF approval since they are not legally considered transfers. ATF does, however, recommend filing tax-free transfer paperwork on all such temporary transfers, to confer an extra layer of legal protection on both the owner and the gunsmith.<sup id="cite_ref-atf-faq_30-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-atf-faq-30">&#91;30&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_conduct">Criminal conduct</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Criminal conduct">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Act makes certain conduct a criminal offense, in relation to engaging in business as a manufacturer, importer, or dealer with respect to (NFA) firearms without having registered or paid a Special Occupational Tax (SOT); receiving or possessing a firearm transferred to oneself in violation of the NFA; receiving or possessing a firearm made in violation of the NFA; receiving or possessing a firearm not registered to oneself in the National Firearms Registration and Transfer Record; transferring or making a firearm in violation of the NFA; or obliterating, removing, changing, or altering the serial number of the firearm.<sup id="cite_ref-35" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-35">&#91;35&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criminal_penalties">Criminal penalties</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Criminal penalties">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Violations of the Act are punishable by up to 10 years in federal prison and forfeiture of all devices or firearms in violation, and the individual's right to own or possess firearms in the future. The Act provides for a penalty of $10,000 for certain violations.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36">&#91;36&#93;</a></sup> A willful attempt to evade or defeat a tax imposed by the Act is a felony punishable by up to five years in prison and a $100,000 fine ($500,000 in the case of a corporation or trust), under the general tax evasion statute.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37">&#91;37&#93;</a></sup> For an individual, the felony fine of $100,000 for tax evasion could be increased to $250,000.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38">&#91;38&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Exceptions">Exceptions</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Exceptions">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The United States Supreme Court has ruled in <i><a href="/wiki/Haynes_v._United_States" title="Haynes v. United States">Haynes v. United States</a></i> that the <a href="/wiki/Fifth_Amendment_to_the_United_States_Constitution" title="Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution">Fifth Amendment to the United States Constitution</a> exempts felons—and, by extrapolation, all other prohibited possessors—from the registration requirements of the Act. The prohibited person who violates the possession prohibition can, however, be convicted under the Gun Control Act of 1968 for being a prohibited person in possession of a (any) firearm.
</p><p>The <a href="/wiki/Atomic_Energy_Act_of_1954" title="Atomic Energy Act of 1954">Atomic Energy Act of 1954</a> was amended in 2005 and includes a provision (42 U.S.C. § 2201a) to allow <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_Regulatory_Commission" title="Nuclear Regulatory Commission">Nuclear Regulatory Commission</a> (NRC) licensees and authorized contractors to possess machine guns for the purpose of providing security.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39">&#91;39&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40">&#91;40&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_market_for_NFA_items">The market for NFA items</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The market for NFA items">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Importation of NFA firearms was banned by the 1968 <a href="/wiki/Gun_Control_Act" class="mw-redirect" title="Gun Control Act">Gun Control Act</a> which implemented a "sporting" clause. Only firearms judged by ATF to have feasible sporting applications can be imported for civilian use. Licensed manufacturers of NFA firearms may still, with the proper paperwork, import foreign NFA firearms for research and development purposes, or for government use.
</p><p>The domestic manufacture of new <a href="/wiki/Machine_gun" title="Machine gun">machine guns</a> that civilians could purchase was effectively banned by language in the <a href="/wiki/Firearm_Owners_Protection_Act" title="Firearm Owners Protection Act">Firearm Owners Protection Act</a> of 1986 (also known as "McClure-Volkmer"). The language was added in an amendment from <a href="/wiki/William_J._Hughes" title="William J. Hughes">William J. Hughes</a> and referred to as the Hughes Amendment.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41">&#91;41&#93;</a></sup> Machine guns legally registered prior to the date of enactment (i.e. May 1986) are still legal for possession by and transfer among civilians where permitted by state law. The static and relatively small number of transferable machine guns has caused their price to rise, often over $10,000, although transferable <a href="/wiki/Mac-10" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac-10">Mac-10</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mac-11" class="mw-redirect" title="Mac-11">Mac-11</a> submachine guns can still be purchased for around $8,000.<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42">&#91;42&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43">&#91;43&#93;</a></sup> Machine guns manufactured after the FOPA's enactment can be sold only to law enforcement and government agencies, exported, or held as inventory or "dealer samples" by licensed manufacturers and dealers. Machine guns made after 1986 for law enforcement but not transferable to civilian registration are usually priced only a few hundred dollars more than their semi-automatic counterparts, whereas a pre-Hughes Amendment registered machine gun that can be legally transferred commands a huge premium.
</p><p>The Hughes Amendment affected only machine guns. All other NFA firearms are still legal for manufacture and registration by civilians under Form 1, and transfer of registration to civilians via Form 4 (though some states have their own laws governing which NFA firearms are legal to own there). <a href="/wiki/Suppressor" class="mw-redirect" title="Suppressor">Silencers</a> and Short Barreled Rifles are generally the most popular NFA firearms among civilians, followed by Short Barrel Shotguns, Destructive Devices, and "Any Other Weapons". While most NFA firearms are bought from manufacturers and transferred to civilians through a dealer, many are made by the civilians themselves after filing a Form 1 and paying the $200 tax. Some types of NFA firearms can be relatively simple to make, such as making a Short Barreled Rifle (by swapping out the upper receiver for one containing a short barrel) or a Short Barreled Shotgun by using a <a href="/wiki/Pipe_cutter" class="mw-redirect" title="Pipe cutter">pipe cutter</a> to shorten the barrel length, while making other NFA firearms, such as Silencers require more technical skill.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2019)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="NFA_trust">NFA trust</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: NFA trust">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An NFA <a href="/wiki/Trust_law" title="Trust law">trust</a> (also known as a gun trust, Title II trust, ATF trust, or Class 3 trust) is a legal trust that is used in the United States to register and own NFA firearms.  Under regulations, use of a trust allows prospective purchasers of NFA items to avoid some of the federal transfer requirements that would otherwise be imposed on an individual.<sup id="cite_ref-nyt1_44-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-nyt1-44">&#91;44&#93;</a></sup> Like other trusts, it allows for <a href="/wiki/Estate_planning" title="Estate planning">estate planning</a> in inheriting firearms. In 2013, ATF proposed new rules,<sup id="cite_ref-:0_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup> often referred to as ATF Proposed Rule 41p, which, if adopted, would require all "responsible persons" of an entity being used to purchase NFA items to comply with the same procedures as individuals in obtaining NFA items.  In an NFA trust, a responsible person is defined as "any grantor, trustee, beneficiary, ... who possesses, directly or indirectly, the power or authority under any trust instrument, ... to receive, possess, ship, transport, deliver, transfer, or otherwise dispose of a firearm for, or on behalf of, the entity."<sup id="cite_ref-:0_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-:0-45">&#91;45&#93;</a></sup>  ATF finalized the rule on January 15, 2016, to become effective 180 days later. The previous requirement for "chief law enforcement officer" approval was eliminated, while all responsible people for a trust must now comply with the same restrictions as individual owners.<sup id="cite_ref-jdsupra1_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-jdsupra1-46">&#91;46&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47">&#91;47&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-guardian1_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-guardian1-48">&#91;48&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Miller_case"><i>Miller</i> case</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Miller case">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1938, the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Western_District_of_Arkansas" title="United States District Court for the Western District of Arkansas">United States District Court for the Western District of Arkansas</a> ruled the statute unconstitutional in <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Miller" title="United States v. Miller">United States v. Miller</a></i>. The defendant Miller had been arrested for possession of an unregistered short double-barreled shotgun, and for "unlawfully ... transporting [it] in interstate commerce from Claremore, Oklahoma to Siloam Springs, Arkansas" which perfected the crime.<sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49">&#91;49&#93;</a></sup> The government's argument was that the short barreled shotgun was not a military-type weapon and thus not a "<a href="/wiki/Militia" title="Militia">militia</a>" weapon protected by the Second Amendment, from federal infringement. The District Court agreed with Miller's argument that the shotgun was legal under the Second Amendment.
</p><p>The District Court ruling was overturned on a direct appeal to the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Supreme_Court" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Supreme Court">United States Supreme Court</a> (see <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Miller" title="United States v. Miller">United States v. Miller</a></i>). No brief was filed on behalf of the defendants, and the defendants themselves did not appear before the Supreme Court. Miller himself had been murdered one month prior to the Supreme Court's decision. No evidence that such a firearm was "ordinary military equipment" had been presented at the trial court (apparently because the case had been thrown out—at the defendants' request—before evidence could be presented), although two Supreme Court justices at the time had been <a href="/wiki/United_States_Army" title="United States Army">United States Army</a> officers during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> and may have had personal knowledge of <a href="/wiki/Combat_shotgun" title="Combat shotgun">the use of such weapons in combat</a>. The Supreme Court indicated it could not take <a href="/wiki/Judicial_notice" title="Judicial notice">judicial notice</a> of such a contention.
</p><p>The Supreme Court reversed the District Court and held that the NFA provision (criminalizing possession of certain firearms) was not in violation of the Second Amendment's restriction and therefore was constitutional.
</p><p>Subsequent rulings have been allowed to stand, indicating that short-barreled shotguns are generally recognized as ordinary military equipment if briefs are filed (e.g., see: <i><a href="/w/index.php?title=Cases_v._United_States&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Cases v. United States (page does not exist)">Cases v. United States</a></i>),<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50">&#91;50&#93;</a></sup> describing use of short-barreled shotguns in specialized military units.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Uncertainty_after_Miller_and_after_passage_of_Montana_House_Bill_246">Uncertainty after <i>Miller</i> and after passage of Montana House Bill 246</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Uncertainty after Miller and after passage of Montana House Bill 246">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The scope of the application of the Act to privately constructed firearms or devices is uncertain. Such items would normally be regulated under the Act's provisions, but are intended for private ownership only and not for sale. As the Act's application is derived from the federal legislature's Constitutionally enumerated power of regulation over interstate commerce, it is unclear how privately constructed firearms or devices built solely for personal possession (i.e., not intended to be delivered into the stream of interstate commerce) are affected by the Act.
It would seem they are regulated under the Supreme Court's interpretation of <i><a href="/wiki/Wickard_v._Filburn" title="Wickard v. Filburn">Wickard v. Filburn</a></i><sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51">&#91;51&#93;</a></sup> which establishes that even activities that occur solely intrastate could have such a substantial effect upon interstate commerce that failure to regulate such commerce would defeat Congress's power to regulate interstate commerce.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>In the landmark 1995 case of <i><a href="/wiki/United_States_v._Lopez" title="United States v. Lopez">United States v. Lopez</a></i>, the first decision in six decades to invalidate a federal statute on the grounds that it exceeded the power of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">United States Congress</a> under the <a href="/wiki/Commerce_Clause" title="Commerce Clause">Commerce Clause</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>, the Supreme Court described <i><a href="/wiki/Wickard_v._Filburn" title="Wickard v. Filburn">Wickard v. Filburn</a></i> as "perhaps the most far reaching example of <a href="/wiki/Commerce_Clause" title="Commerce Clause">Commerce Clause</a> authority over intrastate commerce". The Supreme Court majority that decided the 2005 case <i><a href="/wiki/Gonzales_v._Raich" title="Gonzales v. Raich">Gonzales v. Raich</a></i> relied heavily on <i>Filburn</i> in upholding the power of the federal government to prosecute individuals who grow their own medicinal marijuana pursuant to state law. In <i>Raich</i>, the court held that, as with the home grown wheat at issue in <i>Filburn</i>, home grown marijuana is a legitimate subject of federal regulation because it competes with marijuana that moves in interstate commerce. As the Court explained in Gonzalez:
</p>
<blockquote><p>Wickard thus establishes that Congress can regulate purely intrastate activity that is not itself "commercial," in that it is not produced for sale, if it concludes that failure to regulate that class of activity would undercut the regulation of the interstate market in that commodity.</p></blockquote>
<p>Montana House Bill 246, the <a href="/wiki/Montana_Firearms_Freedom_Act" title="Montana Firearms Freedom Act">Montana Firearms Freedom Act</a>, was signed into law by Governor <a href="/wiki/Brian_Schweitzer" title="Brian Schweitzer">Brian Schweitzer</a> on April 15, 2009, and became effective October 1, 2009. This legislation declares that certain firearms and firearms accessories manufactured, sold, and kept within the state of Montana are exempt from federal firearms laws, since they cannot be regulated as interstate commerce.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52">&#91;52&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53">&#91;53&#93;</a></sup> However, this law does not apply to a firearm that cannot be carried and used by one person, a firearm that has a <a href="/wiki/Caliber" title="Caliber">bore diameter</a> greater than 1 ½ inches and uses <a href="/wiki/Smokeless_powder" title="Smokeless powder">smokeless powder</a>, ammunition that uses exploding projectiles or fully automatic firearms. While it is likely to face a court challenge (and specifically intended to provoke one<sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54">&#91;54&#93;</a></sup>), this Montana law would put firearms accessories such as silencers actually made in Montana, marked "Made in Montana", and sold only to Montana citizens outside federal jurisdiction and not subject to the $200 federal transfer tax.
</p><p>As of April&#160;2013<sup class="plainlinks noexcerpt noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=National_Firearms_Act&amp;action=edit">&#91;update&#93;</a></sup>, similar laws had been enacted by Kansas, Tennessee, Wyoming, South Dakota, Utah, Arizona, Idaho and Alaska and introduced in most other states.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55">&#91;55&#93;</a></sup>
</p>
