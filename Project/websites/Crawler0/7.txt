	<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" >Act of Congress</h1>

<p class="mw-empty-elt">
</p>
<div role="note" class="hatnote navigation-not-searchable">For the law which governs relationships between individuals and the government, see <a href="/wiki/Public_law" title="Public law">Public law</a>.</div>
<p>An <b>Act of Congress</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Statute" title="Statute">statute</a> enacted by <a href="/wiki/United_States_Congress" title="United States Congress">Congress</a>. Acts can affect only individual entities (called private laws), or the general public (public laws). For a <a href="/wiki/Bill_(law)" title="Bill (law)">bill</a> to become an act, the text must pass through both houses with a majority, then be either signed into law by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president of the United States</a> or receive congressional override against a presidential veto
</p>

<h2><span id="Public_law.2C_private_law.2C_designation"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_law,_private_law,_designation">Public law, private law, designation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Public law, private law, designation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Private_Law_86-407.jpg" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Private_Law_86-407.jpg/220px-Private_Law_86-407.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="170" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Private_Law_86-407.jpg/330px-Private_Law_86-407.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Private_Law_86-407.jpg/440px-Private_Law_86-407.jpg 2x" data-file-width="1314" data-file-height="1018" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Private_Law_86-407.jpg" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Private Law 86–407</div></div></div>
<div class="thumb tright"><div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a href="/wiki/File:Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf" class="image"><img alt="" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf/page1-220px-Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf.jpg" decoding="async" width="220" height="337" class="thumbimage" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf/page1-330px-Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/58/Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf/page1-440px-Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf.jpg 2x" data-file-width="946" data-file-height="1447" /></a>  <div class="thumbcaption"><div class="magnify"><a href="/wiki/File:Page_212_from_STATUTE-073-1-2_Public_Law_86-90.pdf" class="internal" title="Enlarge"></a></div>Part of Public Law 86–90</div></div></div>
<p>In the United States, Acts of Congress are designated as either <b>public laws</b>, relating to the general public, or <b>private laws</b>, relating to specific institutions or individuals. Since 1957, all Acts of Congress have been designated as "Public Law X–Y" or "Private Law X–Y", where X is the number of the Congress and Y refers to the sequential order of the bill (when it was enacted).<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1">&#91;1&#93;</a></sup> For example, P. L. 111–5 (<a href="/wiki/American_Recovery_and_Reinvestment_Act_of_2009" title="American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009">American Recovery and Reinvestment Act of 2009</a>) was the fifth enacted public law of the <a href="/wiki/111th_United_States_Congress" title="111th United States Congress">111th United States Congress</a>. Public laws are also often abbreviated as Pub. L. No. X–Y.
</p><p>When the legislation of those two kinds is proposed, it is called <a href="/wiki/Public_bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Public bill">public bill</a> and <a href="/wiki/Private_bill" class="mw-redirect" title="Private bill">private bill</a> respectively.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Usage">Usage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Usage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The word "act", as used in the term "Act of Congress", is <a href="/wiki/Proper_noun_and_common_noun" class="mw-redirect" title="Proper noun and common noun">a common, not a proper noun</a>. The capitalization of the word "act" (especially when used standing alone to refer to an act mentioned earlier by its full name) is deprecated by some dictionaries and usage authorities.<sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2">&#91;2&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3">&#91;3&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4">&#91;4&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5">&#91;5&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6">&#91;6&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7">&#91;7&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8">&#91;8&#93;</a></sup> However, the <a href="/wiki/Bluebook" title="Bluebook">Bluebook</a> requires "Act" to be capitalized when referring to a specific legislative act.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9">&#91;9&#93;</a></sup>  The <a href="/wiki/United_States_Code" title="United States Code">United States Code</a> capitalizes "Act". 
</p><p>The term "Act of Congress" is sometimes used in informal speech to indicate something for which getting permission is burdensome. For example, "It takes an Act of Congress to get a building permit in this town."
</p>
<h2><span id="Promulgation_.28United_States.29"></span><span class="mw-headline" id="Promulgation_(United_States)">Promulgation (United States)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Promulgation (United States)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>An act adopted by simple majorities in both houses of Congress is <a href="/wiki/Promulgation" title="Promulgation">promulgated</a>, or given the force of law, in one of the following ways:
</p>
<ol><li>Signature by the <a href="/wiki/President_of_the_United_States" title="President of the United States">president of the United States</a>,</li>
<li>Inaction by the president after ten days from reception (excluding Sundays) while the Congress is in session, or</li>
<li>Reconsideration by the Congress after a presidential veto during its session. (A bill must receive a <style data-mw-deduplicate="TemplateStyles:r1020198016">.mw-parser-output .frac{white-space:nowrap}.mw-parser-output .frac .num,.mw-parser-output .frac .den{font-size:80%;line-height:0;vertical-align:super}.mw-parser-output .frac .den{vertical-align:sub}.mw-parser-output .sr-only{border:0;clip:rect(0,0,0,0);height:1px;margin:-1px;overflow:hidden;padding:0;position:absolute;width:1px}</style><span class="frac" role="math"><span class="num">2</span>&#8260;<span class="den">3</span></span> majority vote in both houses to override a president's veto.)</li></ol>
<p>The president promulgates Acts of Congress made by the first two methods. If an act is made by the third method, the presiding officer of the house that last reconsidered the act promulgates it.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10">&#91;10&#93;</a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">&#91;<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (June 2020)">failed verification</span></a></i>&#93;</sup>
</p><p>Under the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Constitution" class="mw-redirect" title="United States Constitution">United States Constitution</a>, if the president does not return a bill or resolution to Congress with objections before the time limit expires, then the bill automatically becomes an act; however, if the Congress is adjourned at the end of this period, then the bill dies and cannot be reconsidered (see <a href="/wiki/Pocket_veto" title="Pocket veto">pocket veto</a>). Besides, if the President rejects a bill or resolution while the Congress is in session, a two-thirds vote of both houses of Congress is needed for reconsideration to be successful.
</p><p>Promulgation in the sense of publishing and proclaiming the law is accomplished by the president, or the relevant presiding officer in the case of an overridden veto, delivering the act to the <a href="/wiki/Archivist_of_the_United_States" title="Archivist of the United States">archivist of the United States</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11">&#91;11&#93;</a></sup> After the archivist receives the act, he or she provides for its publication as a <a href="/wiki/Slip_law" title="Slip law">slip law</a> and in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Statutes_at_Large" title="United States Statutes at Large">United States Statutes at Large</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12">&#91;12&#93;</a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-13" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-13">&#91;13&#93;</a></sup> Thereafter, the changes are published in the <a href="/wiki/United_States_Code" title="United States Code">United States Code</a>.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Judicial_review_and_constitutionality">Judicial review and constitutionality</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Act_of_Congress&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Judicial review and constitutionality">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Through the process of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_review_in_the_United_States" title="Judicial review in the United States">judicial review</a>, an Act of Congress that violates the Constitution may be declared unconstitutional by the courts. The judicial declaration of an act's unconstitutionality does not remove the law from the statute books; rather, it prevents the law from being enforced. However, future publications of the Act are generally annotated with warnings indicating that the statute is no longer valid law.
</p>
